Introduction: Multi-Crew Mining Operations

The MISC Mole is a specialized multi-crew mining vessel designed to extract valuable minerals from asteroids and planetary surfaces. With three independent mining turrets and a cargo capacity of 96 SCU, the Mole represents a significant step up from solo mining operations. This Standard Operating Procedure provides comprehensive guidance for operating the Mole efficiently and profitably in Star Citizen 4.5.

What is Mining in Star Citizen?

Mining is the process of extracting valuable minerals from rocks found on planetary surfaces, moons, and asteroid belts. The process involves scanning for deposits, fracturing rocks using mining lasers, and collecting the resulting fragments. Refined ore can be sold at commodity trading terminals for profit.

Why the MISC Mole?

The Mole offers several advantages over solo mining ships like the Prospector:

  • Three Mining Turrets: Allows for coordinated fracturing of larger, more valuable rocks
  • 96 SCU Cargo Capacity: Three times the capacity of the Prospector (32 SCU)
  • Multi-Crew Operation: Enables specialization and efficiency through teamwork
  • Higher Profitability: Can tackle rocks up to 20,000+ mass with proper crew coordination
  • Solo Viable: Can be operated solo by switching between turrets (advanced technique)

Basic Mining Mechanics

Mining in the Mole follows a four-phase process:

  1. Scanning: Use the ship's scanner to identify mineral deposits. High-value deposits appear as bright, stable signals at long range (8km+).
  2. Fracturing: Use mining lasers to apply power to a rock until it fractures. The goal is to keep the charge in the "optimal window" (green zone) while managing instability.
  3. Extraction: After fracturing, use the extraction beam to collect the valuable fragments into the cargo hold.
  4. Refinement: Transport the raw ore to a refinery station, where it is processed into sellable commodities.

Quantanium Timer: Critical Safety Mechanic

⚠ CRITICAL WARNING: Quantanium is an unstable mineral that will detonate 25 minutes after entering your cargo hold. If the timer expires, your ship and cargo will be destroyed.

When mining Quantanium, operators must:

  • Start the timer immediately when Quantanium enters the cargo hold
  • Plan a return route to the nearest refinery before reaching 10 minutes remaining
  • Return to the refinery before the timer expires
  • Avoid "topping off" the cargo hold with additional rocks once Quantanium is aboard

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Chasing Low-Value Rocks: Wasting time on rocks with low ore percentage or inert material. Always check composition before fracturing.
  2. Overcharging Rocks: Applying too much power too quickly, causing the rock to explode and destroying all ore.
  3. Ignoring the Quantanium Timer: Failing to track the 25-minute countdown, resulting in ship destruction.
  4. Poor Crew Communication: Lack of clear communication leads to inefficient fracturing and wasted time.
  5. Topping Off the Cargo Hold: Trying to squeeze in "one more rock" when Quantanium is aboard, risking timer expiration.
  6. Not Using Gadgets: Attempting to fracture high-instability rocks without gadgets, leading to failed fractures.
  7. Ignoring Hazards: Mining in high-risk areas without proper awareness, resulting in pirate attacks or environmental damage.

Ore Selection Framework

Profitability in Mole mining operations is determined by the ability to identify and prioritize high-value targets. This seven-part framework provides a systematic approach to ore selection, ensuring operators maximize their earnings while minimizing wasted time.

1. Scan Return Strength: What to Chase

You do not get a numeric value on the first ping, so use distance and clarity to identify valuable deposits.

✓ Chase These:

  • Return appears beyond ~8 km
  • Bright, stable blip on the scanner
  • Does not flicker when you change heading
  • Often resolves into 2–4 rocks (clusters)

These correlate with mineable mass and high ore concentration.

✗ Ignore These:

  • Only appears inside 3–4 km
  • Faint or flickering signal
  • Resolves into a single pebble

These waste time and yield minimal ore.

2. Rock Mass: The Hard Numbers

Once you scan a rock directly, you get mass in tons. The Mole's multi-crew nature allows it to tackle significantly larger rocks than the Prospector.

Mass Range Crew Requirement Notes
4,000 – 10,000 mass Solo or 2-person Sweet spot for solo operations
10,000 – 15,000 mass 2-3 person Ideal for coordinated crews
15,000 – 20,000 mass 3-4 person Maximum profitability with full crew
<2,000 mass Skip Even if Quantanium is present, yield is too low
>20,000 mass Skip (solo/small crew) Requires 4-person crew with perfect coordination

3. Quantanium Percentage: When to Mine

This is the real gate for profitability. Quantanium is the most valuable ore, but it requires careful management due to the 25-minute timer.

✓ Mine Immediately If:

  • Quantanium ≥30% in a single rock
  • Quantanium ≥25% in a multi-rock cluster

✗ Skip If:

  • Quantanium <20%
  • Quantanium mixed heavily with inert junk (>50% inert)

Remember: A half-full hold of 35% Quantanium beats a full hold of garbage.

4. Other Valuable Minerals

If Quantanium is not present or below threshold, evaluate other minerals:

Mineral Minimum % Value Tier Notes
Taranite ≥40% High Second most valuable, no timer
Hadanite ≥50% Medium-High Consistent value, common on Lyria
Bexalite ≥50% Medium Good backup option
Corundum ≥60% Medium Lower value but common
Titanium ≥70% Low Only if nothing else available

5. Fracture Difficulty & Instability

When you target a rock, you will see:

  • Optimal charge window (green zone)
  • Instability rating (how erratic the charge window behaves)

✓ Green Light:

  • Instability manageable with BoreMax or OptiMax gadgets
  • Charge window not razor-thin after gadgets
  • Multiple lasers can coordinate to manage instability

✗ Red Flag:

  • Instability spikes uncontrollably even with gadgets
  • Charge window collapses even with multiple lasers
  • Rock resistance exceeds combined laser power

Leave. You are not here to wrestle rocks.

6. Quantanium Timer: The Rule That Matters

Once Quantanium enters your hold, the clock is live.

⚠ Critical Timer Rules:

  • You have 25 minutes before detonation
  • Timer stacks with amount collected (more Quantanium = same timer)
  • Heat and damage can shorten the margin

Hard Rules:

  • Start heading back once you hit your target fill (60-80% Quantanium)
  • Do not "top off" the cargo hold
  • Do not detour for one more rock

Target Fill (Safe and Fast): 60–80% Quantanium. This balances payout and survival.

Greed kills runs.

Operator Proficiency Pathways

Proficiency with the MOLE is measured by the crew's ability to work together to efficiently and safely mine high-value deposits. This section outlines the progression from a solo operator to a top-tier mining team, with detailed strategies for each level.

Solo Operator

Operating the MOLE solo is a significant challenge but can be highly profitable. It requires constant seat-switching and a deep understanding of mining mechanics. This is the ultimate test of a solo miner's skill.

  • Focus: Seat-switching efficiency, power management, gadget use, patience.
  • Target Rocks: Stable rocks up to 10,000 mass. Avoid highly unstable rocks.
  • Expected Yield: 100-150k aUEC/hour (highly dependent on operator skill).
  • Key Strategy: Park the MOLE at an optimal distance and angle to the rock. Switch to the main turret, fracture the rock, then switch back to the pilot seat to reposition for extraction. Repeat for each fragment. Use gadgets liberally to manage instability.

Beginner Crew (2 Operators)

A pilot and one dedicated mining operator. This is the entry point for multi-crew mining. The focus is on clear communication and basic coordination.

  • Focus: Clear communication, basic coordination, rock selection, power management.
  • Target Rocks: Stable rocks up to 12,000 mass.
  • Expected Yield: 150-200k aUEC/hour.
  • Key Strategy: The pilot positions the ship and may operate a secondary laser for support. The primary operator focuses on the main fracture, communicating power levels and instability clearly.

Intermediate Crew (3 Operators)

A dedicated pilot and two mining operators. This is the optimal crew size for most mining operations, providing a balance of efficiency and coordination complexity.

  • Focus: Advanced coordination, instability management, multi-laser fracturing, rapid extraction.
  • Target Rocks: Unstable rocks up to 15,000 mass.
  • Expected Yield: 200-280k aUEC/hour.
  • Key Strategy: The pilot focuses solely on positioning and scanning. The two operators coordinate their fire, with one leading the fracture and the other providing support to manage instability and speed up the process.

Advanced Crew (4 Operators)

A dedicated pilot and three mining operators. This configuration allows for maximum mining power and efficiency, capable of tackling the largest and most challenging rocks in the verse.

  • Focus: Maximum efficiency, simultaneous fracturing of multiple rocks, high-risk/high-reward targets, flawless communication.
  • Target Rocks: Massive, highly unstable rocks up to 20,000+ mass.
  • Expected Yield: 280-400k+ aUEC/hour.
  • Key Strategy: The crew operates like a well-oiled machine. All three lasers are coordinated for maximum power and precision. The pilot is constantly scanning for the next target while the operators are fracturing and extracting. This level of operation requires extensive practice and a deep understanding of the MOLE's capabilities.

Execution Doctrine: The Mining Operation

A successful MOLE mining operation is a well-choreographed dance between the pilot and the turret operators. This section provides a detailed breakdown of each phase, including communication protocols and specific actions for both multi-crew and solo operations.

Phase 1: Prospecting

  1. Navigate & Scan: The pilot navigates to the chosen mining location and performs a wide-range scan (ping) to identify potential deposits.
  2. Identify Target: The pilot selects a high-value target based on the Ore Selection Framework and positions the ship for optimal turret coverage.
  3. Communicate Intel: The pilot communicates the rock type, mass, instability, and composition to the crew. Example: "Calling out a 12,000 mass Quantanium rock, 35% concentration, low instability. Positioning on the port side."

Phase 2: Fracturing (Multi-Crew)

  1. Assign Primary: The pilot assigns a primary operator to lead the fracture.
  2. Coordinate Fire: All operators power up their lasers and coordinate their fire on the target. The primary operator initiates the charge, with support operators adding power as needed.
  3. Communicate Status: The primary operator provides a constant stream of information: "Charge started," "Approaching optimal," "In the green," "Instability spike, feathering power," "Fracture complete."
  4. Power Management: The pilot manages ship power, prioritizing lasers during the fracture and shields during extraction.

Phase 2.5: Fracturing (Solo Operation)

Based on the strategies demonstrated by Captain Deadwake, solo operation of the MOLE is a high-skill, high-reward endeavor that relies on efficiency of movement and precise laser control.

  1. Positioning is Key: Park the MOLE at an optimal distance (50-100m) and angle to the rock, ensuring the main turret has a clear line of sight.
  2. Seat-Switching: The core of solo operation. After positioning, switch to the main turret (front) to begin the fracture.
  3. Laser Control: Use a powerful laser like the Helix II for the initial crack. If instability spikes, feather the power or use a gadget.
  4. Gadget Use: Gadgets are essential for solo operation. Use a BoreMax to manage instability and a Stampede to power through resistant rocks.
  5. Reposition & Repeat: After the initial fracture, switch back to the pilot seat to reposition the ship for extracting the fragments. You may need to switch between turrets to reach all fragments.

Phase 3: Extraction

  1. Coordinated Extraction: The pilot positions the ship for efficient extraction. Operators are assigned to specific fragments to speed up the process.
  2. Cargo Management: The pilot monitors cargo capacity and provides updates at 50%, 75%, and 90% full.
  3. Jettison Low-Value Ore: If the hold is nearing full and a high-value fragment is available, jettison low-value ore to make room.

Phase 4: Refinement & Logistics

  1. Navigate to Refinery: Once the cargo hold is full, the pilot navigates to the nearest refinery.
  2. Unload & Refine: The crew unloads the ore and starts the refining process.
  3. Restock & Repair: While the ore is refining, the crew restocks on gadgets, fuel, and medical supplies, and repairs any damage to the ship.

Troubleshooting: Common Failures & Solutions

This section addresses the most common failures encountered during Mole mining operations and provides specific solutions for each scenario.

1. Laser Power Insufficient for Rock Fracture

Symptom: The charge bar fills slowly or not at all, even with all three lasers firing.

Cause: Rock resistance exceeds combined laser power.

Solution:

  • Use a Stampede module to boost laser power
  • Ensure all three lasers are firing simultaneously
  • If still insufficient, skip the rock and find a lower-resistance target

2. Instability Spikes Uncontrollably

Symptom: The charge window fluctuates wildly, making it impossible to maintain optimal charge.

Cause: High instability rating on the rock.

Solution:

  • Use a BoreMax gadget to reduce instability
  • Feather laser power (pulse on/off) to manage the charge
  • Coordinate with crew to balance power across multiple lasers
  • If instability remains unmanageable, skip the rock

3. Quantanium Timer Expired

Symptom: Ship and cargo destroyed by Quantanium detonation.

Cause: Failed to return to refinery within 25 minutes.

Prevention:

  • Start the timer immediately when Quantanium enters cargo hold
  • Plan return route before reaching 10 minutes remaining
  • Do not "top off" cargo hold once Quantanium is aboard
  • Prioritize speed over additional mining once timer is active

4. Thermal Overheating of Mining Lasers

Symptom: Lasers shut down due to overheating.

Cause: Sustained high-power laser use without cooldown periods.

Solution:

  • Upgrade to IceBox (Industrial A) cooler for better thermal management
  • Rotate laser use between turrets to allow cooldown
  • Reduce laser power during extraction phase

5. Power Plant Insufficient for Sustained Mining

Symptom: Ship systems shut down or lasers lose power during fracturing.

Cause: Power plant cannot supply sufficient energy for all three lasers simultaneously.

Solution:

  • Upgrade to Cirrus (Industrial A) power plant
  • Manage power distribution: prioritize lasers during fracture, shields during extraction
  • Reduce non-essential systems during mining operations

6. Shield Failure During Mining Operations

Symptom: Shields collapse, exposing ship to damage.

Cause: Pirate attack or environmental hazards.

Solution:

  • Upgrade to 7SA 'Concord' (Civilian A) shield for maximum protection
  • Maintain situational awareness: scan for hostile ships regularly
  • Retreat to safe distance if under attack
  • For multi-crew: assign one operator to monitor shields and threats

7. Cargo Hold Overflow/Management

Symptom: Cargo hold full, but high-value fragments remain.

Cause: Poor cargo management or unexpected high-yield rock.

Solution:

  • Jettison low-value ore to make room for high-value fragments
  • Monitor cargo capacity throughout extraction phase
  • Prioritize Quantanium and Taranite over lower-value ores

8. Navigation/Approach Vector Failures

Symptom: Ship positioned poorly for turret coverage, requiring constant repositioning.

Cause: Poor initial positioning or pilot inexperience.

Solution:

  • Position ship 50-100m from rock at optimal angle for all three turrets
  • Use decoupled mode for fine positioning adjustments
  • For solo: park ship before switching to turret

9. Communication Breakdown (Multi-Crew)

Symptom: Crew members work at cross-purposes, wasting time and resources.

Cause: Lack of clear communication protocols.

Solution:

  • Establish clear roles: pilot, primary operator, support operators
  • Use standardized callouts: "Charge started," "In the green," "Fracture complete"
  • Pilot provides constant intel: rock mass, composition, instability
  • Practice together to build coordination

10. Rock Explodes (Overcharge)

Symptom: Rock detonates, destroying all ore.

Cause: Charge exceeded optimal window and entered red zone.

Solution:

  • Feather laser power as charge approaches optimal window
  • Use OptiMax gadget to widen charge window
  • Coordinate crew to reduce power simultaneously when approaching red zone
  • Practice charge management on low-value rocks before attempting high-value targets

  • Navigated to nearest refinery (ARC-L1, CRU-L1, MIC-L1, HUR-L1)
  • If Quantanium aboard: Arrived at refinery with time remaining on timer
  • Unloaded ore at refinery
  • Started refining process
  • RESET TIMER after ore unloaded
  • Restocked mining gadgets
  • Refueled ship
  • Repaired any damage
  • Collected refined ore and sold at commodity terminal

Performance Tracking

  • Recorded total SCU mined
  • Recorded ore types and percentages
  • Recorded total aUEC earned
  • Calculated aUEC per hour
  • Identified areas for improvement
